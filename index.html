00 — The thesis

Australia will get its AI productivity dividend. The Productivity Commission judges multifactor productivity gains above 2.3% likely over the coming decade — roughly $116 billion in additional GDP. The contested question is not whether AI creates wealth, but who captures it.

This forecast argues that on current policy settings the dividend flows disproportionately to capital owners and to the high-skill labour that AI complements, opening a distribution gap: the wedge between the wealth AI generates and the share reaching the middle-skill workers whose tasks it automates. The gap is not inevitable. It is the product of one mechanism — automation reduces the labour share in value added (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2019) — interacting with a second that locks it in: a concentration ratchet, through which early-adopting, data-rich incumbents compound their advantage. Both are reversible by policy.

The horizon is 2025–2030: near enough to extrapolate credibly from current adoption data, far enough for structural change to register.

The dividend's existence is settled. Its distribution is the contingent, policy-sensitive variable — and it is measurable.

01 — Four causal channels

How a capability becomes a distribution

The dividend reaches workers — or doesn't — through four channels. The first three were the heart of the conventional account; the fourth, industry structure, is the one that turns a transient shock into a permanent gap.

Channel 1 · Tasks — where exposure lands

The dividend begins as a task story. Generative AI is competent at routine cognitive work — drafting, reconciling, classifying, retrieving — so exposure concentrates where those tasks cluster. Jobs and Skills Australia, scoring 998 occupations, finds automation potential highest among middle-skill clerical roles at ANZSCO skill level 4 — General Clerks, Accounting Clerks, Bookkeepers — while higher-skill professionals skew toward augmentation. The pattern follows the routine-task logic of Autor, Levy and Murnane (2003): codifiable, repetitive tasks are the substitutable ones.

International evidence agrees on the locus — the ILO's refined index places data-entry clerks near 0.70 mean exposure, accounting clerks near 0.64 — though the finding that higher-income jobs face greater exposure derives from US data (Eloundou et al. 2024). Exposure is not socially neutral: women hold about 70% of clerical and administrative roles, and General Clerks' automation potential alone touches an estimated 3.7% of employed women (JSA 2025). With roughly 450,000 people employed in financial and insurance services — a sector JSA ranks among the most exposed — the affected population is large in absolute terms, not marginal.

Channel 2 · Wages — why the productivity–wage link bends

Exposure becomes a wage story through the labour share. Because automation always reduces the labour share in value added and can depress labour demand even while raising productivity (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2019), the dividend accrues first to capital and to complemented high-skill labour. That is the engine of the gap. Yet direction is not predetermined: generative AI raised the productivity of low-skill support workers by 14–35% while barely moving top performers — a compression of the skill premium that inverts the historical skill-biased pattern. Whether wages polarise or compress depends on access, not capability. The decoupling is still latent: the producer-wage–productivity link 'remains intact' for the 95% of workers outside mining and agriculture, yet real wages already sit around 6% below their mid-2020 peak — the gap has somewhere to open from.

Channel 3 · Employment — the displacement–reinstatement race

Whether exposure becomes lost jobs depends on a race between displacement and reinstatement — the creation of new labour-demanding tasks (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2019). Where reinstatement lags, the result is 'so-so technology': enough automation to displace workers, too little new work to re-absorb them. Crucially, in Australia this channel is still latent. Among firms making group retrenchments in 2024, only twelve cited generative AI or related technological change; entry-level ad shares have held stable since 2022 — 'large-scale job displacement is yet to emerge'. The largest figure in circulation — McKinsey's estimate that up to 1.3 million workers may need to transition by 2030 — is modelled potential, not prophecy.

Channel 4 · Industry structure — the concentration ratchet

The fourth channel turns a shock into a structural gap. AI adoption rises with firm size and data: among SMEs using generative AI, only 29% deploy it in core activities, while large firms integrate it deeply. Because data is the scarce complementary input, incumbents that capture the dividend first reinvest in data and model advantage, deepening a moat SMEs cannot cross — and the advantage compounds each period. The concentration is also spatial: high-skill AI employment clusters heavily in Sydney and Melbourne. As concentration rises, the labour share falls structurally. In financial services — the most exposed white-collar industry, and one where APRA already tracks structural concentration — the dynamic is clearest: a handful of data-rich incumbents dominate, and AI raises the entry barrier further. This is why the gap, once open, does not self-correct.

02 — The signature mechanism

The concentration ratchet

The ratchet is the heart of this forecast. Watch the advantage compound: each year, data-rich incumbents convert the dividend into a deeper data moat, and SMEs fall further behind — unless a data-access right lets them keep pace. Run it both ways.

Read across the three, the difference is not the size of the dividend but its destination. Drift and Active Transition can post almost identical headline productivity; what separates them is whether the labour share holds near 59% or slides below 57% — the same dividend, opposite distributions. The wargame makes the point operationally: the trajectory is set less by how much AI can do than by whether policy keeps the data input contestable and reinstatement funded.

What would change this conclusion
  1. A labour share below ~57% or Gini above 0.31 by 2028 → the downside is realised (Treasury 2017; ABS 2022-23).
  2. Visible net new middle-skill task creation in JSA's entry-level ad-share monitoring → the augmentation case holds.
  3. MFP gains below ~0.5% — the PC's $26 billion floor — would put the dividend itself, not merely its distribution, in doubt.
04 — The policy playbook

Assign the right, don't redistribute the dividend

The problem is distributional, but the classical-liberal response is institutional, not redistributive. Coase (1960) showed that where property rights are clearly assigned and transaction costs are low, externalities resolve through bargaining rather than transfers. Applied here, the gap is best addressed by assigning rights over the ratchet's key input — data — and by keeping AI-input markets contestable, rather than by taxing the dividend. This also aligns with the Productivity Commission's blueprint, which treats AI-specific regulation as a last resort.

Three risks attach, each with a real mitigation, not a restated problem. A data-access right could raise privacy and transaction costs — the mitigation is tiered, portable consent rather than blanket disclosure. Competition intervention could itself become a productivity drag — the mitigation is an ex-post, last-resort posture (PC 2025) rather than ex-ante prohibition. And reskilling money can be spent without effect — the mitigation is funding released against re-employment outcomes, monitored by JSA, precisely because Australian effectiveness data is thin.

The political economy explains the inertia. Incumbents have concentrated incentives to resist data-access reform; the SMEs and displaced workers who would benefit are diffuse and unorganised — the classic collective-action asymmetry. That asymmetry has an equity dimension: with women holding around 70% of the exposed clerical roles (ABS 2024), the cost of inaction falls unevenly. The Commonwealth Bank's early-2026 workforce repositioning — a staff-redeployment program announced alongside AI-driven redundancies — shows the ratchet operating in real time.

Tax the dividend and you fight the symptom every year. Assign the data right once and you slow the ratchet that produces it.

05 — Methods, data & limits

How this was built

This forecast extrapolates from current adoption and exposure data rather than predicting discrete events, in the spirit of scenario 'wargaming'. Throughout, figures are tagged: a solid underline marks a sourced number; a dotted underline marks an assumption. Several limitations are material and disclosed below.

JSA exposure scores are point-in-time potential estimates, not forecasts, and its CGE modelling is described as illustrative; no single scalar exposure score exists for financial services as an industry, so it is cited directionally.

The latest official Gini is 2022-23 (0.307), because the ABS Survey of Income and Housing 2023–24 will not be released.

Australian job-polarisation evidence is mixed — strong in the 1980s–90s (Coelli & Borland 2016) but weak thereafter (Wilkins & Wooden 2014) — so the US polarisation narrative is not assumed to transfer.

The Acemoglu–Restrepo robot elasticities are US industrial-robot estimates, used here as a flagged proxy. SME-vs-large adoption rates diverge by an order of magnitude across surveys and are treated as sensitivity-tested assumptions.

Many headline figures use 'could' or 'may' (PC, Tech Council, McKinsey) and are treated as modelled potential, said so in-text.

AI-tool disclosure. AI was used to structure, source-check and pressure-test the argument and to build the interactive components. The economic reasoning, scenario design and policy logic are the author's own.
