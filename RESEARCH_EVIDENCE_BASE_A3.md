## 0. THE CORE MECHANISM (the spine of the forecast)

The causal chain that underwrites the thesis, end to end:

**AI capability** (LLMs now competent at routine cognitive / information-processing tasks)
→ **task-level exposure concentrated in middle-skill clerical, administrative and financial-clerical roles** (JSA 2025; ILO 2025; Eloundou 2023)
→ **automation displaces those tasks while augmenting high-skill professionals** (JSA: automation concentrated at ANZSCO skill level 4; augmentation at levels 1–2)
→ because **automation "always reduces the labour share in value added"** (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2019), the productivity gain accrues first to **capital owners and complemented high-skill labour**
→ **absent reinstatement (new-task creation) and structural policy** (competition, data access, reskilling), the dividend is captured disproportionately = **the distribution gap**.

The dividend is real and quantified (PC's 2.3%+ MFP / $116bn; Tech Council's up to $115bn). Its *distribution* is contingent on policy and on whether reinstatement keeps pace with displacement.

## 1. AREA 1 — Australian occupational AI exposure

**Central Australian source — Jobs and Skills Australia (2025), *Our Gen AI Transition: Implications for Work and Skills* (Generative AI Capacity Study Final Report, 14 August 2025, Canberra).**
- Scored 998 four-digit ANZSCO occupations on automatability and augmentability (0–1 scales), adapting the ILO method (Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond 2023) and Felten et al. (2021), using ABS ANZSCO v1.3 and Census 2021.
- **Headline:** ~4% of the current workforce are in occupations with *high* automation exposure to Gen AI; 79% have *low* automation exposure. Augmentation outweighs automation overall; largest single group is 49% of workers in low-automation / medium-augmentation occupations.
- **Middle-skill concentration (thesis core):** potential to automate tasks is highest for middle-skill occupations, particularly ANZSCO skill level 4 (clerical work). Higher-skill occupations (levels 1–2) skew toward augmentation. "The higher potential for automation is concentrated in routine clerical and administrative roles."
- **Financial services link:** in JSA's CGE modelling, the three-digit occupations projected to lose most employment by 2050 are General clerks; Receptionists; and Accounting clerks and bookkeepers. Financial and insurance services is among the five industries with greatest modelled employment losses. Female employment in finance is high in low-growth, highly exposed roles (Accounting Clerks, Bookkeepers, Payroll Clerks, Bank workers).
- **Distributional detail:** General Clerks' automation exposure would affect 3.7% of employed women, 3.1% of workers with disability, 2.5% of the Indigenous workforce, 2.6% of workers aged 55+. Women marginally more exposed to both automation and augmentation.
- **Caveat:** exposure scores are point-in-time *potential* estimates, not forecasts; CGE modelling is "illustrative"/"stylised". No single scalar exposure score is published for Financial & Insurance Services as an industry — cite directionally.

**Indeed Hiring Lab Australia (16 April 2024), *20% of Australian Jobs Face a High Potential Exposure to Generative AI*.**
- ~21% (one in five) Australian job postings face "high exposure" (GenAI can perform ≥80% of required skills at good/excellent level); a further 56% at moderate exposure.
- By contrast, GenAI can perform just 29% of skills required in a driving occupation, 39% for nurses.

**ILO — Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond (2025), *Generative AI and Jobs: A Refined Global Index of Occupational Exposure*, ILO Working Paper 140.**
- Clerical occupations have highest exposure globally: data entry clerks ~0.70 mean exposure; accounting/bookkeeping clerks ~0.64.
- Earlier ILO WP96 (Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond 2024): 24% of clerical tasks highly exposed, 58% medium — far above any other occupational group.

**Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin & Rock — *GPTs are GPTs*.**
- Working paper (2023, arXiv:2303.10130): ~80% of US workforce could have ≥10% of tasks affected by LLMs; ~19% could see ≥50% affected; higher-income jobs face greater exposure.
- Published version (2024), "GPTs are GPTs: Labor market impact potential of LLMs," *Science* 384(6702):1306–1308: 1.8% of jobs with >half tasks affected, rising to ~46% with complementary software.
- **[US data — proxy for Australia.]** Cite the 80%/19% headline to the 2023 working paper; the 1.8%/46% framing to the 2024 *Science* article.

## 2. AREA 2 — Productivity / TFP forecasts and trends 2025–2030

**Productivity Commission (2025), interim report *Harnessing data and digital technology* (5 August 2025).**
- "Multifactor productivity gains above 2.3% are likely over the next decade (0.23 percentage points annually) … this would imply labour productivity growth of about 4.3% … about an extra $116 billion of GDP (2023-24 prices) over the next decade."
- Even a 0.5% MFP gain implies "about $26 billion in additional GDP."
- Better personal-data access could deliver productivity gains worth as much as $10 billion a year.
- Companion research papers: PC (2025), *Making the most of the AI opportunity*.

**Tech Council of Australia & Microsoft (2023), *Australia's Generative AI Opportunity*.**
- GAI could contribute $45bn (slow), $75bn (medium), or $115bn (fast) annually by 2030 — equivalent to 2–5% of the economy; ~70% of value from worker productivity gains; most gains ($30–80bn) from automating routine tasks.

**Tech Council of Australia, Microsoft, LinkedIn & Workday (2024), *Meeting the AI Skills Boom* (2 July 2024).**
- "AI has the potential to create up to 200,000 jobs in Australia by 2030"; AI workforce to grow 500% over seven years (~800 workers in 2014 → >33,000 in 2023).

**Historical slowdown.**
- RBA (2023, *Recent Trends in Australian Productivity*): productivity growth averaged 2.1% from 1990s–mid-2000s, falling to ~1.2% over the 2010s.
- RBA (2025): market-sector labour productivity averaged ~0.6%/yr 2017/18–2023/24 vs 1.6% over the prior two decades; medium-term productivity assumption cut to ~0.7%.
- Treasury, *Intergenerational Report 2023*: lowered long-term productivity-growth assumption from 1.5% to 1.2%/yr (down from 1.75% in the 2002 IGR). A 0.3ppt lift to 1.5% would raise real GDP ~9.5% and wages ~8.5% by 2062-63.
- RBA chief economist Sarah Hunter (October 2025): cut sustainable average wage-growth estimate from ~3.5% to ~3.2%, citing the productivity downgrade.

## 3. AREA 3 — Who captures the gains (the distribution gap)

**Labour share.**
- Treasury (2017, *Australian productivity trends and the effect of structural change*): capital's share of market-sector factor income ~41% (off a 2011-12 peak of 44%) → labour share ~59%.
- RBA (La Cava 2019, *The Labour and Capital Shares of Income in Australia*; RDP 2018-10): long-run decline driven partly by housing/financial-sector capital income and within-industry shifts. ABS (2018): 83% of the decline was within-industry.

**Job polarisation.**
- Coelli & Borland (2016), "Job Polarisation and Earnings Inequality in Australia," *Economic Record* 92(296):1–27 — polarisation occurred but predominantly in the 1980s–1990s.
- Wilkins & Wooden (2014): little polarisation 2003–2013.
- **Caveat:** Australian polarisation evidence is mixed and weaker than the US — do not overstate.

**Wage–productivity decoupling.**
- Productivity Commission (2023), *Productivity growth and wages – a forensic look*: for the 95% of the workforce outside mining and agriculture, decoupling is "relatively low"; productivity outstripped producer-wage growth by 0.18%/yr; the underlying link "remains intact."
- ABS (2023, *Has worker compensation reflected labour productivity growth?*): a 1.0ppt rise in labour productivity historically produced ~0.8ppt rise in real compensation (market sector, 1994/95–2021/22); some net decoupling since 2011-12.

**Acemoglu & Restrepo task framework (the causal channel).**
- "Robots and Jobs: Evidence from US Labor Markets," *Journal of Political Economy* 128(6), 2020: "One more robot per thousand workers reduces the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2 percentage points and wages by 0.42%" (range 0.18–0.34pp; 0.25–0.5%). **[US industrial-robot data — proxy.]**
- "Automation and New Tasks: How Technology Displaces and Reinstates Labor," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 33(2), 2019: automation "always reduces the labor share in value added and may reduce labour demand even as it raises productivity"; offset is the "reinstatement effect." "So-so technologies" bring sizeable displacement but modest productivity gains — the worst case for labour.

**Income inequality.**
- ABS, *Income and wealth inequality*: Gini for equivalised disposable household income 0.307 in 2022-23 (down from 0.323 in 2021-22; vs 0.299 in 2002-03); household-wealth Gini 0.606 in 2022-23. Australia had 16th-highest income inequality of 37 OECD countries (2022).
- **Data gap:** the ABS Survey of Income and Housing 2023–24 will not be released → 2022-23 is the latest official Gini.

## 4. AREA 4 — Scenario calibration data (baseline / upside / downside)

**Regional concentration.**
- CSIRO & Tech Council of Australia (2023), *The geography of Australia's digital industries*: four tech "super-clusters" — Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Canberra. Sydney arc holds 81 ASX-listed digital firms ($52bn); Melbourne diamond 62 firms ($203bn). High-skill AI/tech jobs heavily concentrated in Sydney and Melbourne.

**SME vs large-firm adoption.**
- ABS (2022): ~1% AI adoption. Later Australian surveys diverge widely: MYOB 2024 (19%), NAB 2024 (23%), PwC 2024 (25%), Ai Group 2024 (52%), NAIC Jan–Jun 2025 (27%).
- OECD (2025, *AI adoption by small and medium-sized enterprises*): among SMEs using generative AI, only 29% use it in core activities; adoption rises with firm size and digital maturity.
- **Caveat:** official Australian series is the ABS "Business use of digital technology"/Business Characteristics survey. The order-of-magnitude divergence (1% to 52%) means adoption rates must be tagged as assumptions and sensitivity-tested.

**Reskilling capacity.**
- JobTrainer Fund (Dept of Employment and Workplace Relations): $2bn Commonwealth–state program; as of December 2022, 562,000+ enrolments and 206,000+ completions, including ~10,000 digital-skills places.
- **Data gap:** enrolment/completion counts exist; rigorous outcome/effectiveness evaluations are sparse — flag as requiring an explicit assumption.

**Real wages.**
- ABS Wage Price Index (analysed by MacroBusiness, Nov 2025): Australian real wages tracking 6.0% below their June 2020 peak as of June quarter 2025 — roughly the December 2011 level. RBA (Feb 2026) forecasts real wages still ~6.4% below the mid-2020 peak by June quarter 2028.

**Displacement to date.**
- JSA (2025): among Australian firms reporting group retrenchments (15+ employees) in 2024, only 12 cited Gen AI or related technological change; "Large-scale job displacement is yet to emerge." Entry-level job-ad share stable since 2022.
- McKinsey (2023, *Generative AI and the future of work in Australia*): "up to 1.3 million workers—9 percent of Australia's total workforce—may need to transition out of their current roles into new occupations by 2030"; up to ~850,000 from disrupted/declining office-support, production, food-service and sales roles. **[Modelled potential — "may"; not a realised forecast.]**

## 5. AREA 5 — Chris Berg's positions and Coasean / institutional framing

- **Chris Berg** — Professor of Economics, RMIT; co-founder, RMIT Blockchain Innovation Hub (formerly Adjunct/Senior Fellow, IPA). Author of *Artificial Intelligence: A Classical Liberal Approach* (Springer, 2025); *Institutional Acceleration* (Cambridge UP, 2025); *Understanding the Blockchain Economy* (Edward Elgar, 2019); *The Classical Liberal Case for Privacy* (Palgrave, 2018).
- Applies the institutional economics of **Coase and Williamson** — a property-rights and transaction-cost lens, not a redistributive one.
- Public-policy stance: deregulation ("reduce red and green tape, remove barriers to employment, cut tax rates"); market-oriented competitiveness over redistribution; critical of technology regulation as a productivity drag.
- Aligns with the **Productivity Commission's 2025 market-oriented blueprint**: AI-specific regulation as "last resort," pausing economy-wide "Mandatory Guardrails for high-risk AI," expanding data access/competition over redistribution.
- **Coase (1960), "The Problem of Social Cost," *Journal of Law and Economics* 3:1–44:** externalities resolvable through clearly assigned property rights and bargaining where transaction costs are low. Applied to AI: address distributional concerns via competition policy and data-access rights (cf. PC's data-portability proposals) rather than direct redistributive transfers — the natural Bergian framing for the policy section.

## 6. SCENARIO CALIBRATION (sourced anchors vs tagged assumptions)

| Scenario | Productivity anchor (SOURCED) | Distribution outcome (mix of sourced + ASSUMPTION) |
|---|---|---|
| **Baseline — "Drift"** | PC (2025): MFP +2.3% / labour productivity +4.3% / +$116bn GDP over the decade | Labour share drifts from ~59% (Treasury); Gini holds near 0.307 (ABS) but wealth Gini elevated at 0.606; gains concentrate in Sydney/Melbourne clusters (CSIRO/TCA 2023). *Displacement magnitude = ASSUMPTION, calibrated to JSA middle-skill exposure.* |
| **Upside — "Active Transition"** | Tech Council/Microsoft (2023): up to $115bn/yr fast-adoption (2–5% of GDP) | Reinstatement (A&R 2019) keeps pace; reskilling absorbs displaced clerical workers (JobTrainer capacity, with effectiveness rate = ASSUMPTION); data-access/competition policy spreads gains to SMEs. |
| **Downside — "Concentration"** | RBA (2025): ~0.7% productivity assumption + A&R "so-so technology" | Labour share falls below ~57% (ASSUMPTION); Gini rises materially above 0.31 (ASSUMPTION); robot-style elasticities (−0.2pp employment, −0.42% wages, A&R 2020 — US proxy) applied with tagged adjustment. |

**Benchmarks that would shift the conclusion:**
- (a) Labour share below ~57% OR Gini materially above 0.31 by 2028 → downside / "so-so technology" path realised.
- (b) Net new middle-skill task creation visible in JSA entry-level ad-share monitoring → augmentation-dominant case holds.
- (c) MFP gains below ~0.5% (PC's $26bn floor) → the dividend itself, not just its distribution, is in doubt.

## 7. CAVEATS & DATA GAPS (for the Methods section)

- JSA exposure scores are *potential* estimates, not forecasts; CGE modelling is "illustrative."
- No single scalar JSA exposure score for Financial & Insurance Services as an industry — cite directionally.
- ABS Survey of Income and Housing 2023–24 not released → 2022-23 (Gini 0.307) is the latest official inequality datapoint.
- Australian job-polarisation evidence is mixed (Coelli-Borland 2016 vs Wilkins-Wooden 2014); the US narrative does not transfer cleanly.
- Acemoglu-Restrepo robot elasticities are US industrial-robot estimates, not Australian GenAI — use as a calibrated, tagged assumption.
- Reskilling-program *effectiveness* data is thin — flag as an explicit assumption wherever scenarios rely on reskilling capacity.
- Many headline AI figures use "could"/"may" (PC, Tech Council, McKinsey) — treat as modelled potential, say so in-text.
- SME-vs-large adoption rates diverge by an order of magnitude across surveys — tag and sensitivity-test any adoption assumption.
